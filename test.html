The Chalet

Known locally as 'the hobbit hut', this cosy getaway is fully equipped and very comfortable. We guarantee you will get a good night's sleep, whatever the weather.

  • Sleeps two in twin beds
  • Sun room
  • Kitchen with gas hob, microwave, fridge
  • Shower room
  • Central heating and constant hot water
  • Sunny patio with garden furniture and barbecue
  • Off-street parking
  • Bedlinen and towels supplied
  • Television and WiFi
  • Large garden
  • Pet friendly

The coast path runs behind the chalet and the beach is literally 100 metres away. Park on the drive, switch your phone off and listen to the sounds of birdsong and the lullaby of waves breaking on the beach, then slip into the turquoise waters of the sea and come home to a hot shower.

  • The Cove

The Cove

Trevaunance is Cornish for a homestead in a wooded valley. The Cove is at the mouth of a long wooded valley which snakes down to the sea half a mile from the centre of St Agnes village. The beach is beautiful, sheltered by high cliffs, popular with local families and surfers alike, there are Lifeguards in the season. You can explore the sea-caves, rockpools, the ruined harbour and, for the athletic, a walk across the rocks at low tide takes you to Star Beach and Trevellas Cove. Seals and seabirds are a common sight, as well as occasional visits from turtles, porpoises, dolphins, sunfish and basking shark. Surfing lessons and kayak tours are available on the beach.

Much of the television series, ‘Poldark’ was filmed in this area: 11 kilometres of the St Agnes coastline are designated as Heritage Coast and the Cove is an ideal centre to explore the north Cornwall coastal path, or follow the mining trail from Wheal Coates on the cliffs at nearby Chapel Porth to the Blue Hills tin stream at Trevellas Cove (next door to Trevaunance Cove). Please note that there are strong currents at Trevellas Cove and it is not suitable for bathing.

The area is also a coastal Site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI) Cornwall, England, UK, due to its interesting flora, fauna and biological and geological characteristics. A number of rare and scarce plant species can be found, along with many breeding seabirds, such as black legged kittiwakes, butterflies, dragonflies and damsel flies.

And if that’s not enough, the area is also designated as an AONB – area of outstanding natural beauty which also merits a place on the Dark Skies Map as St Agnes and Chapel Porth are some of the best star-gazing locations in the UK. Just stand outside on a clear night and look up towards the west for breath-taking views of the Milky Way Walk. Or you can drive to the small car park off Beacon Drive (SW 699513) and climb the Beacon for an awe-inspiring view of the night sky.

Apart from the natural features, the Cove is a relaxing, fun place with plenty of interest. There is a RNLI lifeboat station, fishing catches are landed on the slipway, Breakers is a great beach café and there is also a restaurant/bar on the beach. Up Quay Road you’ll find a fish and chip shop and the famous 17th century pub, the Driftwood Spars, originally a fish cellar, many of its original timbers came from shipwrecks. If you carry further on up Quay Road, you’ll find a small arts and crafts complex and two tea rooms.

  • St. Agnes

St. Agnes

Situated on the north coast somewhere between Newquay and St Ives, St Agnes is a hidden gem, highly rated in the Sunday Times as one of the 3 best places to live and holiday (17/03/2017).

St Agnes is a proper village with all the facilities you need: two butchers, two fruit and veg shops (with organic produce), two mini markets, a pharmacy, bakery, hairdressers, library, museum, craft/art studios, a village hall, flower shop, jewellers, restaurants, pubs and cafes, as well as superfast broadband, wonderful coastal walks and great surf. There are many talented artists producing arts, crafts, locally made jewellery on display in the village craft shops and the studios in Quay Road. Many of the restaurants specialise in fish and seafood.

St Agnes is a thriving community, with lots going on, live music in at least one of the pubs, amateur dramatic productions as well as fixed celebrations such as Giant Bolster Day (May Day), the pageant of St Agnes and the famous 'Blood Dance', Victorian Fayre day (end of May), the Carnival (first Saturday in August) and Lifeboat Day (August).
